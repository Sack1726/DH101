## Research Documentation
1.	What sources did you use?
The sources I used mostly consisted of official documentation from OpenAI & Google and from investigative journalism. I did consult Kate Crawford’s Atlas of AI, but I found that while it talked about related topics, it did not give a very specific location and began to make the process ten times longer. Local regional investigative news sources like Oregon Public Broadcasting’s article on The Dalles Google data center location were especially helpful in getting a better perspective on how locations affect populations.
2.	What was hardest to find? (This reveals what's hidden)
The hardest sources to find without a doubt were mining locations and generally any company that had a third-party connection to the AI. The reasons for this might be groundbreaking for the naive, but for the more authentic minds, it is very straightforward. The many processes and conditions that are involved in these practices are dangerous, harmful, and amoral.
When reading some reports, a picture started to paint in my head that many of these instances are not intentionally committed but rather are committed from a lack of concern for ethics. This happening is not from some encouraging political economic systems like capitalism or socialism/communism too. It is the desire rooted in modern hedonism that puts a profit of any kind above all else. 
The result of this desire that thirsts for more results takes advantage of what has already been established in the world. Humans have a lot of hubris. People might refuse to see it in themselves or act like it only impacts a certain group, but it does not remove the desire from the world.
3.	What surprised you?
There was not a huge specific aspect that was surprising to me when researching. The part I would say that was most surprising is the fact that many investigative news articles that expose harmful conditions of some places are locked behind paywalls. While a headline itself can be enough to gain attention, I find it ironic and counterproductive to try to raise awareness of an issue while giving it a price.

## Artist Statement
1.	What argument does your map make?
My map makes an argument that AI is run and developed in First World countries while Second World and Third World countries provide raw resources and plain labor. The United States being the place of origin of AI is seen in the map as well with headquarters being in the Silicon Valley area. In other words, this map makes the argument that AI was founded there. In addition, the wide range of resources that are tapped into reflect the corporate nature of AI development. Without this global often overlooked infrastructure, AI would not be the same in its form. As a matter of fact, this extends to many tech products as AI itself takes advantage of a pre-existing larger tech infrastructure.
2.	What would be different if you mapped something else?
If I mapped something else like other tech products that are not AI, the map might show other prominent labor hotspots that cultivate in revealing the world of manufacturing. The results might be similar with major corporations, however, the locations might appear wider as these corporations are global entities with many markets. AI has specifically appeared to a more First World audience with a significant lack of infrastructure for poorer regions.
3.	How does this connect to Data Feminism's ideas about power and visibility?
My map definitely reflects pre-existing power structures that developed over time. Much of AI’s development is centered around wealthy First World tech corporate areas that might make other places seem invisible even in the same country. The hidden infrastructure with the Second World and Third World reveals a map that is not necessarily invisible but is not often talked about.
