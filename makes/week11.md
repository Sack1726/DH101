## Research Notes 
- What sources did you use?
I first used the Washington Post article “The Cobalt Pipeline” by Todd Frankel. His article opened the door to many similar articles featuring the life of Congo cobalt miners. The only problem I had was to choose what to include. I figured the best way to find sources was to find ones that kept the voice of the cobalt miners who are often invisible. The best sources I found were all investigative journalism, but I consulted opinions from government sources to balance out the exaggerated emotions often associated with journalism.
- What was hardest to find?
There are many articles that feature different opinions from outsiders but rarely directly featured the cobalt miners’ opinion or side. It was harder to find exact quotes from miners instead of just a paraphrase from a researcher or journalist. I think this compromises the true invisible aspect of these laborers in exchange for potential mischaracterization. I eventually resulted in asking Gemini for suggestions toward sources which helped less than I expected as it hallucinated quotations or gave quotations from journalists treating it like a miner. 
- What surprised you?
The aspect that surprised me the most were some of the political dynamics at play in the Democratic Republic of the Congo and with outsiders. I did not include much of this research in the zine and focused more on general dangers to appeal to a wider audience. The part that particularly intrigued me was the influence of mercenaries in cobalt mining in the Congo and beyond. I am not sure to what extent this is true, but I started to feel as if the Cold War never ended in some of these regions, especially for mining. It felt ironic to me that some of these mercenary groups depicted themselves as liberators from a socialist regime when they themselves were the ones who brought it there. Regardless, a status quo of mining remains despite any political effect.


## Design Statement 
- Why did you choose this format?
I chose a zine-like infographic because it felt the most rewarding format to practice. I have made text-heavy info reports before, but trying to prepare a way to present a lot of information in a limited space presented more challenges than I expected. What do I choose to keep, reference, or summarize? Even though I did not use AI to make the infographic, this process reminded me of the previous make 8. How I found information in articles can be compared to how AI generated and presented content. For both, it was up to me to choose and filter what I was trying to convey.
- Who is your audience?
My audience would be first-worlders who are unaware of the exact cobalt mining happening for technologies like AI. These individuals would want an overview of the events and how it connects to their activities but also do not want to be info dumped on a bunch of information that overwhelms them. I tried to keep it basic with a focus on the miners themselves.
- What do you want them to feel/do?
Since it is hard to answer what the best way to respond to this problem is, I wanted my audience to understand the perspective of the cobalt miners more specifically. This is why I have an entire page dedicated to a few direct quotes from miners themselves. I also blurred all the faces of the cobalt miners to garner respect towards these individuals. Without it, I felt readers would try to judge their facial expressions too harshly which would detract from the infographic’s purpose.
