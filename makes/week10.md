## Design Statement
- What is your game about?
My game is based on Concept C: A Day in the Life. It follows a college student who awakens to find an uncompleted paper on AI adoption into militaries and only a day to finish it. The college student is assisted (or annoyed) by an AI assistant named Cognoco who readily follows any distraction of the student. The name Cognoco is taken from the Latin roost cogn meaning “to know” and co meaning “together.” The optional “sleeping” section quotes an edited paragraph from one of my published anthology works that deal with memento mori. With both endings in the game being negative, this part, if hard to understand, references the unavoidable nature of AI in the future. It is an unavoidable reality that must be faced.
- What choice(s) matter? Why?
The beginning sections with the “sleeping” and linear narrative serve as a sort of small tutorial of the game's actions. This quickly changes in the beginning when reaching the “Morning Work” section. One difference in between the following parts can completely change the outcome. There are only two endings in the game and neither of them are good. Each choice that leads into each ending being “Afternoon Work” and “Order Half” have multiple choices to instantly switch tracks between endings.
- How do the rules/mechanics support the theme?
The endings themselves lead to the player’s downfall, either caused by the AI Cognoco or by the player himself. This causes the player to try to find other ways to find an ending, but none will be found which is by design. AI will continue to play a role in the future. It cannot be avoided nor relied on fully to replace actions some may deem it can. To not repeat myself from the first paragraph, I had different good endings or outcomes from choices but decided to remove them in order to reinforce the unavoidable future nature of AI for people.

## Artist Statement 
- What did you learn about games as a form?
Comprehensive games with unique characteristics cannot be easily made with AI. Oftentimes, any game made heavily from AI can be easily spotted. There are simply too many aspects of video games like visuals, audio, story, and technicality. I see AI being used for placeholder parts in games, but with the state of AI today, there are simply too many assets for a complex video game. There might come a time when AI is good enough to generate a game that seems almost indistinguishable from a human made work, however, this is still limited by its inherent limitations like limited memory, hallucinations, and statistical patterned thinking.
- How did AI collaboration work for you?
I used no AI for the text or story of the game, but used Visual Studio Code to make my ink file into a visible game. The exact prompts I used were:
1.	“If I gave you the ink file could you create something with it?
2.	“You can make a browser game.” <attached ink file>
3.	“Can you add backgrounds and visual elements to it? Infer from text and use stock photos online and insert it into the background. Add transitions between options.”
Following this, I practically argued with the AI over dozens of prompts, asking things like “...put the text choices at the bottom,” “Have both Endings have the same image,” and “Remove speaker bubbles from each text box.” I would say that making and writing the story in Inky was the hardest part, but arguing with Visual Studio Code lasted many more hours until I found it acceptable.
- What would you do differently?
I definitely want to add more choices and better images than just stock photos to the game. I originally was going to make an entire video game section that lightly played into a wider story with the Cognoco AI but quickly scrapped it to avoid going off-topic. My choice to use stock photos instead of AI images results from me wanting more control of the game through choosing pre-made real images, not from AI.
