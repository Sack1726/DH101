## Network Analysis 
- What is the most central node? Why?
The most central node in the graph is the paper “Does AI Affect the Democratic Conduct of War? Analyzing US and Israeli Military AI Deployment” by Alessandra Russo. Russo’s argument contrasts with other arguments by authors in that she takes a much more realistic and practical perspective on AI regulation. Most papers discussed large legal frameworks for AI in the military, but Russo argues that focusing on short-term behavioral norms for AI is much more feasible. This resulted in different critiques to and from Russo with other arguments by authors.
- What clusters/groups emerged?
❖	Strategic Analyzes — Each paper published in the Journal of Strategic Studies focuses on what value (if any) AI challenges or brings into military decisions.
❖	AI Escalation  — James Johnson and papers connected to his work surround concerns about unintentional escalations of force if AI makes decisions in a military setting. 
❖	Risks & Measures — The paper “AI and International Stability: Risks and Confidence-Building Measures” by Michael C. Horowitz and Paul Scharre form a cluster of connections that argue for and against AI risk in the inevitable future.
- What was surprising?
What surprised me is how I could summarize the arguments for AI regulation in militaries into two camps. First, given military capacities, focusing on short-term goals to implement beneficial AI functions is more effective in the long run. Second, a large extensive legal regulatory framework is possible with enough planning despite worries of instability at times. In other words, each camp’s argument favors either a less or more direct regulation for AI in military settings.
- What is MISSING from this network?
All my research was in English and received little attention to non-Western audiences. It was mainly NATO militaries that were being concerned about without much regard to any outsider organizations. In addition, exact corporate AI companies like Anthropic, Google, or OpenAI were mentioned but barely discussed at all. Finally, most sources came from a humanities focused environment, lacking specific technical backgrounds like computer science or data science.

## Artist Statement 
- What does your network reveal about power? Who is visible? Who is invisible?
As similarly stated in the last section of the network analysis, there was little attention given to non-Western audiences. It was not as if this audience did not exist but rather almost disregarded in the discussion of regulating AI in a military setting. I think this reveals a problem frequently seen with countries that hold dominant power. Policymakers whether political, academic, or both open hegemonic narratives that often marginalize groups of people making them invisible. In this case, these invisible people are blind spots within AI regulation for military applications. 
Sun Tzu in The Art of War says “If you know yourself but not the enemy, for every victory gained you will also suffer a defeat” (Tzu 3:18). Each institution, journal, and researcher in my network appear to know themselves from a regulatory perspective but do not know their contrasting people who are made invisible. These invisible people are non-Western actors around the world, AI scientists/engineers who develop the AI itself, and the tech companies that manage its services.
- How would a different network structure tell a different story?
If a different network structure such as one that includes more sources and connections to the invisible people mentioned in the previous paragraph, then the story surrounding AI regulation in the military would fit a more global network. However, I would reckon that a network focusing too much on the invisible people may flip the dynamic between those who are visible and invisible, removing broader socio-political context required in discussing AI military regulation. Additionally, new invisible individuals may be created. This dynamic could be a reason for some fragmented relationships between governments and AI companies.
