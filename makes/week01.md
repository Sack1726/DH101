## 1. What Is Being Made?
ORBIS reconstructs a computational model of travel across the Roman Empire around 200 AD. The output is an interactive map model that produces the cost of travel in the Roman world by simulating movement across networks while also taking into account season and means.
## 2. What Is the Project Made From?
Data is Roman communication networks, consisting of 632 sites. This data comes from a composite of antiquity sources, environmental information, and historian scholarship. What is missing is unpredictable obstacles that cannot be well accounted for by a generalized modal.
## 3. Tools, Algorithms, or Systems
ORBIS uses mechanisms like GIS frameworks and Dijkstra's algorithm, used to find the shortest path between two nodes in a weighted graph. I could find no mention of AI being used for anything in the process. The process is automated through a computer model, but there is historical thought inserted in data.
## 4. Human Labor & Decisions
Historians, archaeologists, and geographers collected, cleaned, and categorized data which looked for relevant or significant information to interpret into arranged data sets. Human judgment shapes the outcome mainly by any historical assumptions.
## 5. Design as Argument
ORBIS maximizes user options with its interface allowing for different conditions to be selected to generate routes. The project makes it possible to see how some regions in the Roman Empire had easier connections at different conditions than others. What makes the model hard to see is within its limitations like the social disruptions and natural hazards.
## Assignment Reflection
In this project, the “making” consists of inputting a computational model of the Roman world that is functionally defined by historical evidence and scholarly thinking which culminates in a presented graph. Data containing important sites, routes, and historical assumptions of the Roman Empire is translated into a data structure that presents a graph that takes a look into how items and information traveled. Therefore, the “making” here is heavily computed. When machines participate in making, a lot of processes that were previously unrealistic or even impossible, become much more foreseeable. Without the computational model participating, the process to graph the fastest, cheapest, or shortest path within the Roman Empire would be extremely time-consuming. There might not be enough data in one spot or enough time to calculate thousands of possible routes with multiple conditions like the season. Authorship does become harder to see over the computational model’s structured ordered process, however, it is critical to note that there are still limitations that it cannot account for. Social influences are hard to see as and include the reasons, motivations, goals of travelers in the Roman World. People did not and still do not always take the shortest route to their destination.
