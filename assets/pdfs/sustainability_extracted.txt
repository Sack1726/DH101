 1. My Research (brief summary of what you found) 
 ●  Researching AI energy and carbon costs proved difficult, as most articles recycle 
 unverified numbers without solid sourcing. Ten sources were used — primarily 
 academic, nonprofit, and governmental — including a May 2025 paper from a University 
 of Rhode Island, University of Tunis, and Providence College research group. The 
 hardest data to pin down was per-prompt watt and carbon usage, since AI companies tend 
 to significantly underreport their figures compared to independent studies. Even the data 
 ultimately I used carried notable uncertainty, with some standard deviations as high as 
 3.663.

 4. Systemic vs. Individual: 
 - What can individuals do? 
 ●  There are a few things an individual can do to reduce their environmental impact of AI 
 use. First, be sure for whatever purpose you are using AI for that it cannot be done at a 
 more simple level. Second, keep prompts barebones without filler words. Just give details 
 or the question with as much detail for as little amount of words as possible. Third and 
 most importantly, remember your impact! Oftentimes, individuals realize a way they can 
 conduct a behavior better but never do. 
 - What requires systemic change? 
 ●  The usual suspects are required to change. There needs to be more real green energy, a 
 focus on efficiency-first AI modeling, and data center regulations to protect natural 
 resources. Besides this, what needs to be changed is the public attitude & perception 
 toward AI impacts. This can only happen if people are told it exists. 
 - Who is responsible? 
 ●  While each person does have a degree of responsibility, AI companies, governments, and 
 AI programmers hold a significant responsibility that has the most widespread impact.
