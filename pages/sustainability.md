## 1. My Research (Brief Summary)

Researching AI energy and carbon costs proved difficult, as many articles recycle unverified numbers without solid sourcing.

Ten sources were used, primarily academic, nonprofit, and governmental, including a May 2025 paper from a University of Rhode Island, University of Tunis, and Providence College research group.

The hardest data to pin down was per-prompt watt and carbon usage, since AI companies tend to underreport their figures compared to independent studies. Even the data ultimately used carried notable uncertainty, with some standard deviations as high as 3.663.

## 4. Systemic vs. Individual Responsibility

### What Can Individuals Do?

There are several ways individuals can reduce the environmental impact of AI use:

- Make sure AI is necessary for the task and cannot be done more simply.
- Keep prompts concise and avoid filler language.
- Remember that individual behavior matters and follow through on better practices.

### What Requires Systemic Change?

There needs to be more real green energy, greater emphasis on efficiency-first AI modeling, and stronger data-center regulation to protect natural resources. Public understanding of AI environmental impacts also needs to improve, which requires better communication and transparency.

### Who Is Responsible?

While each person has some responsibility, AI companies, governments, and AI programmers hold major responsibility because their decisions have the broadest impact.
